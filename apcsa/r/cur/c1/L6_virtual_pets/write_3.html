1. A pet could start out as a baby, and over many correct interactions get older, start to talk, and so forth. Remember, babies basically need to be fed, have their diaper changed, sleep, and be played with. As they get older, things get more complicated.

  2. The pet could be a student in school, with behaviors such as "take test", "snubbed by best friend", "pizza day at cafeteria", "tell joke that whole class laughs at", and so forth.

  3. Your pet could be a famous celebrity, with moods that only a celebrity could think were appropriate. Behaviors might include "attend oscars", "dump rich boyfriend", "sleep in late", and so forth. Think about what kinds of interesting interactions can happen based on the celebrities state of mind (i.e., instance variable values).

  4. A pet could allow many different eating behaviors, covering all different types of food. The pets moods ("upset stomach", "bloated", and so forth) would depend on whether the meal was balanced, it got desert when it wanted it, and so forth. Think of this pet as a nutritional tutorial.

  5. A pet could act out a story, one where the reader has to make choices about how to proceed. The 'moods' are different points in the story (e.g., "at the moving staircase. Its dark here."), and the behaviors are different actions the 'reader' can take (e.g., "Use a 'see in the dark' spell"). If they do the right actions in the right sequence, they advance the story to the conclusion; a wrong behavior might reach a deadend.

    Note: Later in the course, we'll code a really intricate adventure game.

  6. Along the lines above, the pet could be playing in a game (e.g., soccer, chess, and so forth). The moods would be states of the game (e.g., "A tall defender looms up in front of you"), and the behaviors actions the player takes (e.g., "shoot on the goal").
