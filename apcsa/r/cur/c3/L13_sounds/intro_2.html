What a sound is, then, is characterized by the quickly varying pressure in the air. To keep a record of a sound is keep a record of the pressure.

The most common way to keep a record of the pressure is to take many, many individual measurements in rapid succession. As typically stored on compact discs, for instance, each measurement is stored as an integer, varying between –32768 and 32767. This number is called an individual sample. The size of this number is directly related to the amount of pressure in the air: the bigger the value of the sample, the higher the pressure that's recorded. Java could measure the pressure with much more precision than this, but experience shows that this range is usually good enough.

How quickly are samples taken? For compact discs, there are 44,100 samples taken every second. This is called the sampling rate, and the quality of the recorded sound is quite dependent on it. In this lesson, we'll use a sampling rate of 22,050, exactly half that used on compact discs. (Your teacher, or you, may change this default depending on how fast your computer is.)

So, one second of sampled sound is, as far as your computer is concerned, a collection of about 22,000 integers. We'll use ArrayLists to store this.
