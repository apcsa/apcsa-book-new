Taking our cue from the science in Computer Science, let's define sound as a physical phenomenon. We hear sound when there are quickly changing waves of pressure in the air. Think of a wave as a cycle of high and low pressure. So, when someone hits a key on a piano, waves of high air pressure radiate outwards. If you are standing still (or moving at less than the speed of sound, about 1100 feet per second), your ears will "feel" the air pressure get higher and lower and higher and lower again and again.

These cycles are happening quite quickly, sometimes thousands of times a second. How quickly the cycles are repeating is heard as the frequency, or pitch, of the sound. The frequency says how high or low the sound is. The lowest sounds we can hear are cycling on the order of 20-30 times per second. With cycles that slow, you can also feel the sound in your body.

Another way sounds differ is by how much pressure there is in the air or, more precisely, how big a difference there is between the highest and the lowest pressure of the air within a cycle. This is called the amplitude. Big differences in pressure are heard as loud sounds; slight differences in pressure are heard as quiet sounds. And, of course, a constant pressure isn't heard at all—that's complete silence!
