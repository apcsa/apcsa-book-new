In this lesson, we explored algorithms for two kinds of algorithms: shuffling ("un-sorting") and sorting. Shuffling is of course useful for any kind of card game. Sorting is perhaps the most frequently occurring algorithm in any computer applications.

The idea of repeated selection—find and store the smallest, find and store the next smallest, and so on—turns out to be useful both for shuffling and for sorting ("selection sort" occurs frequently enough to be identifiable by name).

  1. Initialize shuffledSoFar to empty.
  2. If myCards is nonempty, perform the shuffling.
    1. Choose a random card from myCards.
    2. Remove it from myCards and add it to the end of shuffledSoFar.
    3. Repeat this process until no more elements remain in myCards.
  3. Finally, replace myCards by shuffledSoFar.
  1. Initialize sortedSoFar to empty.
  2. If myCards is nonempty, perform the sorting.
    1. Choose the lowest-ranking card from myCards.
    2. Remove it from myCards and add it to the end of sortedSoFar.
    3. Repeat this process until no more elements remain in myCards.
  3. Finally, replace myCards by sortedSoFar.
